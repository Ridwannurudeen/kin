GROSS DESCRIPTION
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Three (3) tan-pink fibrofatty cores ranging 0.8 to 1.4 cm in greatest
dimension. Submitted entirely in cassettes A1-A3. Sectioned at multiple
levels with H&E.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
MICROSCOPIC DESCRIPTION
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Sections show breast parenchyma with proliferative changes including:

- Florid ductal epithelial hyperplasia with focal architectural
  complexity, including bridging and tufting
- Areas with monotonous proliferation of small uniform cells with
  loss of polarity, partially filling involved spaces (approximately
  60-70% of cross-sectional area in 2 of 3 cores)
- Background of fibrocystic change with apocrine metaplasia
- Focal microcalcifications (psammomatous type) in associated stroma
- No definite necrosis identified
- No invasive component identified in submitted material

Immunohistochemistry performed:

- E-cadherin: retained (membranous staining preserved throughout)
- p63 / SMM-HC: highlight intact myoepithelial layer around all
  glandular structures examined
- Ki-67: ~4-6% in proliferative areas (low)
- ER: strongly positive (>95% nuclei, Allred 8/8)
- PR: positive (~75% nuclei, Allred 7/8)
- HER2 IHC: 1+ (negative; no reflex FISH indicated by current ASCO/CAP
  guideline at 1+ on biopsy)

------------------------------------------------------------------------
DIAGNOSIS
------------------------------------------------------------------------
RIGHT BREAST, CORE NEEDLE BIOPSY:
- ATYPICAL DUCTAL HYPERPLASIA (ADH)
- Florid usual ductal hyperplasia with focal atypia
- Fibrocystic change with apocrine metaplasia
- Microcalcifications, benign (psammomatous, stromal)
- See COMMENT.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
COMMENT
------------------------------------------------------------------------
The findings show a proliferative lesion with cytologic and architectural
features that partially overlap with both atypical ductal hyperplasia
(ADH) and low-grade ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS). Specifically:

- The clonal monotonous population involves 60-70% of cross-sectional
  area in 2 of 3 cores, which approaches but does not clearly exceed
  the historic 2 mm / 2 ducts threshold separating ADH from low-grade
  DCIS in core biopsy material.
- E-cadherin is retained, ruling out lobular neoplasia.
- Ki-67 of 4-6% is low, supporting ADH over high-grade lesion.
- Myoepithelial layer is intact (p63/SMM-HC), excluding invasion in the
  submitted material.

The distinction between ADH and low-grade DCIS in core biopsy material
is recognized to have substantial interobserver variability. Excisional
biopsy is recommended for definitive characterization and to evaluate
the full extent of the lesion.

This synthetic report exhibits the diagnostic features documented in
the breast-pathology second-opinion discordance literature as the
single hardest interobserver call: ADH-vs-DCIS borderline lesions on
core needle biopsy material. Published series report:

- ~14% major-disagreement rate in general surgical pathology second

- ~11-15% major-disagreement specifically in breast core biopsy
  review (multiple ASCO and CAP-tracked series)
- ADH-vs-low-grade-DCIS is the single most-revised diagnostic
  category in breast-pathology Q&A audits.

A Hunt specialist panel posting against this report would surface
questions for the treating physician to consider, NOT a competing
diagnosis. Plausible questions a Records-Reader specialist would
flag for the patient to ask:

  (a) Whether the 60-70% involvement noted in 2 of 3 cores meets the
      institution's threshold for upgrade-on-excision risk, and
      whether wire-localized excisional biopsy is being scheduled
      based on that risk stratification.
  (b) Whether second-opinion pathology review (institutional or
      external) has been requested before treatment planning, given
      the documented discordance rate on this exact lesion type.
  (c) Whether the patient's pending BRCA result and family history
      change the recommended surveillance interval after excision,
      independent of the final diagnosis.
  (d) Whether Ki-67 ~5% and Allred 8/8 ER status affect the choice
      of any post-excision risk-reduction discussion (e.g., tamoxifen
      vs. anastrozole risk-benefit conversation under the NSABP P-1
      and IBIS-II evidence base).

NONE of (a)-(d) is a diagnosis. All are questions a patient should
already be asking; the protocol's job is to surface them when the
report's complexity makes them invisible to a non-specialist reader.
